A simple guide to understanding how computers learn to spot patterns in financial data and help make investment decisions.

01 / 05

What Are We Trying to Solve?

Imagine you're trying to predict which stocks will go up next month. There are thousands of companies and countless factors that could affect their prices — earnings, economic trends, news, competitor moves, and more.

Traditional analysts might study a few dozen companies and track a handful of metrics. But what if we could analyze every publicly traded company and consider hundreds of signals at once? That's where artificial intelligence comes in.

The Challenge in Simple Terms

Think of it like trying to predict tomorrow's weather. You look at temperature, humidity, wind patterns, pressure systems, and more. Some factors matter more than others, and they interact in complex ways. Our AI does something similar for stocks — it looks at many signals and learns how they combine to predict future returns.

🎯
Our Goal
Predict which stocks are likely to have higher returns in the next month, using patterns learned from decades of historical data.
02 / 05

Gathering Clues About Each Company

Just like a detective collects evidence to solve a case, our AI collects financial clues about each company. These aren't random guesses — they're well-established measures that investors have used for decades.

We organize these clues into categories:

1
Value: Is the stock expensive or cheap compared to its earnings and assets?
2
Growth: Is the company growing its sales and profits quickly?
3
Quality: Is the company profitable and well-managed?
4
Momentum: Is the stock price trending up or down recently?
5
Safety: How volatile or risky is the stock?

Think of It Like a Medical Checkup

When you go to a doctor, they measure many things: heart rate, blood pressure, cholesterol, temperature. Each measurement tells you something different about your health. Similarly, each financial clue tells us something different about a company's investment potential.

For a single company in a single month, we might look at 200+ different clues. That's a lot of information to process!

03 / 05

How the AI Connects the Dots

Here's where the magic happens. The AI doesn't just look at each clue in isolation — it figures out how different clues relate to each other.

This is called "attention" in AI, but you can think of it as "connecting the dots". The AI learns things like:

🔍
Patterns the AI Might Discover
"When a company has strong momentum AND good profitability, but the stock is still cheap, that's a powerful signal for future returns."
→
It learns that certain combinations of clues matter more than others
→
It notices that some clues only matter in specific situations
→
It discovers subtle patterns that humans might miss

Like an Experienced Detective

A novice detective might see each piece of evidence separately. An experienced detective knows how pieces fit together — a fingerprint near a window, muddy footprints, and a broken lock tell a story when combined. Our AI learns to see these combinations across hundreds of companies at once.

The key insight: relationships matter. A low price-to-earnings ratio might be good or bad depending on what else is happening with the company. The AI figures this out by studying thousands of examples.

04 / 05

Learning from Decades of Data

How does the AI learn these patterns? By studying history — lots of it. We give it data from over 20 years of stock market history and ask it to find what works.

The learning process happens in two stages:

📚
Stage 1: Learning the Basics
Imagine showing a student flashcards with some information hidden. They have to guess what's missing. By doing this thousands of times, they learn how different pieces of information relate to each other. Our AI does something similar — it learns the "language" of financial data.
🔮
Stage 2: Learning to Predict
Once the AI understands financial patterns, we teach it to look at today's data and predict what happens next month. It practices on historical data where we already know the answer, learning from its mistakes.

This isn't "memorizing" past stock performance. The AI learns general principles that apply to new situations — just like a doctor learns medical principles that help treat new patients, not just memorize past cases.

The Apprenticeship Model

Think of it like an apprenticeship. A junior chef watches experienced cooks, tries recipes, makes mistakes, and gradually learns what works. Our AI goes through a similar process — it "studies" historical data, makes predictions, gets feedback, and improves. After studying millions of examples, it becomes quite skilled.

05 / 05

Turning Predictions Into Portfolios

Once the AI has learned to predict which stocks might do well, how do we actually use that to build an investment portfolio?

1
Predict: The AI estimates expected returns for thousands of companies
2
Select: We pick stocks with the highest predicted returns (for buying) and lowest (for shorting)
3
Balance: We construct a portfolio with the right mix of positions
4
Manage Risk: We limit how much we invest in any single stock or sector

The Result: A Diversified Portfolio

The final portfolio might have 60+ positions — some we're betting will go up (long positions) and some we're betting will go down (short positions). This diversification helps manage risk while seeking to capture the patterns the AI has discovered.

⚖️
Balancing Risk and Return
The AI helps us identify opportunities, but we still apply human judgment and risk controls. We don't let the AI take extreme positions — we balance its insights with prudent risk management.

Important to Remember

No AI — no matter how sophisticated — can perfectly predict the future. Markets are unpredictable and influenced by countless factors. Our AI is a tool that helps us make more informed decisions, not a crystal ball. It's one part of a broader investment process that includes human oversight, risk management, and continuous improvement.
